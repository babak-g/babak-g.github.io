This past weekend, my girlfriend and I visited her sister and her boyfriend living in Arcata, CA. I found this weekend to be very introspective and I questioned a lot of "what I value" in cites.

Growing up in big, urban cities most of my life, I never really considered why I value the things I do. 

- What do I value about Los Angeles?
- What do I value about Rochester?
- What do I value about Seattle?
- What do I value about Arcata?

I think I've innately rated cities solely based off my family my whole life. Sure ... weather, demographics, and other things were additional factors I considered, but they were never a primary reason as to why I moved somewhere. Staying in Arcata for just 3 days was so informing on what I like/don't like. There's a very high chance I am biased since I like change and putting myself in different environments, but here were a couple things I noticed:

1) Community

Walking around a small community made me feel warm, recognized, and a sense of belonging. Having conversations with those near me either walking around the block or at grocery stores was really nice. Seeing up-to-date flyers of events this month in the town square, supporting and eating fresh, local produce. I never realized how nice this was. 

2) City Infrastructure

Being around  walkers, cyclists, & runners. The ability to hop on your bike, go to work followed by grocery shopping to get fresh ingredients everyday sounded so refreshing. This was something my girlfriend's sister does quite frequently throughout the week.

3) Outdoors

Nature literally being your backyard. Stepping out every morning and breathing fresh air was so therapeutic. Hearing nothing but birds chirp and the wind blow, while looking at hundreds of redwoods was something I never thought I'd enjoy. 

How was I supposed to know these were things I even liked? Do I even like them? Would I like living there for X years? I don't know, but regardless I know I liked it this past weekend.

Something I found super interesting was how my girlfriend's sister and her partner found Arcata, CA. It's so small and them both being from large, urban cities, what was their reasoning for their move? They made a list of things that were either: very important, important, good, and nice-to-have. They went through various cities that they considered and picked a city that scored high on their individual scales. Each category had various points, example:

- Very Important: 15 points
- Important: 9 points
- Good: 3 points
- Nice-to-have: 1 point

## Person A

| Categories      |       Values     |    Arcata, CA   |   Boston, MA   |
| --------------- | ---------------- | --------------- | -------------- |
| Small Community |       9          |    1            |            0   |
| Local Produce   |       15         |    1            |            0   |
| Sports Team     |       1          |    0            |            1   |
| --------------- | ---------------- | --------------- | -------------- |
| Total           |                  |    24           |            1   |


Person A's score for Arcata would be 24 and 1 for Boston. Then Person B would do the exact same thing. Lastly, they would compare and find the highest values.

Yesterday, my girlfriend and I thought it would be fun to do a similar exercise, but instead we allocate X amount of points (i.e. 100 points). Then we distribute our points however we like amongst all the categories we found important. Here were the categories we came up with:

- Native Language 
- Airport Distance
- Grade School Education                  
- Public Transit
- University Education                    
- Outdoor Hikes
- Jobs Salary                             
- Beaches
- Safety                                  
- Age Demographic                           
- Weather [specify ideal temp]            
- Work Culture
- Diversity                               
- Nightlife
- Restaurants                             
- Events
- Local Produce                           
- Sports
- Grocery Stores                          
- Cost of Living
- Driving                                 
- Gyms
- Bike-ability                            
- Distance to Family/Friends
- Walk-ability                            
- Healthcare
- Taxes                                   
- Political Demographic
- Population Size                         
- Volunteer Opportunities

This allows us to scale each category respectively to each other. Example: I don't care about Nightlife, so that would get 0 points on my list, but English being the native language is very important, so I may weight that accordingly. I think we could improve this by allocating more points and/or sharing precises measurements for each category, but my girlfriend brought up a good point that it's hard to map our values to numericals, but we can try.

Here's an example for a subset of the categories listed above. Let's just use 10 points for simplicity.

## Person A

| Categories      |       Values     |    Portland     |   NYC          |
| --------------- | ---------------- | --------------- | -------------- |
| Safety          |       7          |    6            |            4   |
| Bike-ability    |       3          |    2            |            1   |
| Nightlife       |       0          |    0            |            0   |
| --------------- | ---------------- | --------------- | -------------- |
| Total           |                  |    8            |            5   |


This method allows Person A to not value categories they don't care about, and allows them to rate each category per city to their own scale. NYC is a super bike-friendly city to most, but in this instance - Person A values it as a 1/3.

In the future, this is something my girlfriend and I will try out on a few cities. Maybe I'll post an example if we actually get to it, but only time will tell.

Lastly, thank you, Arcata, CA.
