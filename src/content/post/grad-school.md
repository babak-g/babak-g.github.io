## Introduction

Have you heard the saying, if you're hesitant about making a decision. Let it sit and decide after X days/weeks/years have past. Well, years have a past and this wanting to pursure higher education has been sitting rent free in my head. Sort of... 

I'll first start with what degree I want to pursure has drastically varied over the past couple of years, so that's cheating in my opinion. About 2 years ago, I probed the idea of an M.B.A. 

## Why MBA?

This answer also changed over time. At first, I wanted an MBA for maybe not the best reasons. I was ashamed of having gone to a school that doesn't have much of a name. Getting an MBA from an M7 would immediately change that. Some of my reasonings as to why get an MBA [in no order of importance]:

1) I no longer would have to say "I'm Bobby, I went to the University of Rochester. A small school in upstate New York that isn't that same as RIT." I thought it would feel good saying a school name that wouldn't warrant a second sentence. 
2) I would think this would open doors in my career. I asked myself - how to stand out amongst other FAANG engineers who majored in Computer Science. I thought this degree would hold value in that "I did my time." Other MBA grads and older folks in industry would recognize this.
3) The nEtWoRk. I may meet my future business partners and be able to reach out to alumni easily.
4) I think I'd have a great time, be able to make friends who were similar-minded & driven, who I wouldn't meet otherwise.

These reasons weren't enough to convince me to commit to a program. 

I'll translate this situation to something my realtor told me on buying my first home, which I've started to apply to all aspects of my life. Narrow what you want. Find the area that you want to live in, then look through all the homes. When I first started the home-buying process, my circle of interest was from San Diego to San Fernando Valley. First, I needed to ask specify which area suited what I wanted the most and then choose from the houses in that circle. Similarly to getting an MBA, my mind was all over the place. I didn't have the direction on why I specifically needed an MBA. For example, when I walk across the stage and receive that piece of paper, what career is unlocked that I wasn't able to get before in pre-MBA Babak era. I had no answer.

Not to say that these reasons aren't why you shouldn't go get an MBA, but I wanted to introspect on what I wanted out of my career, which I honestly never really thought of before.

Fast forward a couple months, I still thought about getting an MBA. I went hard on researching. I found commencements from top MBA schools from 5,10,15 years ago, and LinkedIn searched so many names on that e-brochure. I was looking for people who came from a technical background and was curious to see what they were up to now. An amount of them started start-ups [that I didn't look closely into to see how successful they were], but most of them converted to Product Managers. The largest commonality was that they all stopped coding, by sitting more at the business x technical layer. 

I even reached out to someone who I was interested in their career because I saw them as a foreshadow of myself. I asked him a lot of questions about on the reasonings as to why he converted from SWE IC -> top MBA business school and to explain his thought process & reasoning in his decision-making. I'll start off by saying that I really liked him and thought he was exceptionally nice, but I found this conversation more along the lines of "Go get an MBA, stop being a free-lunch rat" vs. "These were the reasons as to why I went to get an MBA and why I don't regret it". 

A couple weeks after this, I stopped the thoughts of getting an MBA, since I didn't think my reasonings were strong enough to pursue it. Before I transition to the next degree I want, I want to emphasize that other people's thoughts on opinions on whether you should or shouldn't get an MBA aren't important. I asked so many SWEs around me, and given the field, so many of the answers were extremely quantitative, observable, and trade-off heavy. Which are excellent decision-making skills, but I think one thing in the SWE trade-off equation that I will gladly say is also a fault of mine is that we're too risk-averse. We calculate too much. Sure, two years salary + MBA cost + relocation + future promotion/career progression, etc are all accounted for in deciding the worth of the degree, but not one person I talked to mentioned anything about happiness. As cliche as it sounds, let's not forget what we most care about and is 2 years going to be that huge detriment in gross income outcome? That can a fun conversation for my next team lunch ... well, if we don't talk about the best trifecta of credit cards again.

## CS Masters

After all the reading on communication I've been doing recently, my interest in sentiment analysis has skyrocketed. Do I want to do research and work in a think-tank? I don't think so. But I will say I get excited thinking about going to school in a state where I'm excited to learn.

I've never felt this before. My relationship with school has always been negative and I've never, ever wanted to learn anything. Now, I love reading and I take classes at a local community college that I thoroughly enjoy. Reasonings as to why get a CS Masters in NLP:

1) I can confidently say this is something I'd be super interested in learning and specializing in industry [which is something I wouldn't be able to do w/o this degree...at least not easily] and I can test the waters to see if I want to pursue this more deeply w.r.t. research.
2) I can live outside the US! There are so many good CS programs outside of the US that can quench my thirst to live in another country.
3) I can meet others that I definitely would never meet otherwise, and expand my friend group/network.
4) This would be a field I'd be happy to specialize in my career, and I see the impact on how this work impacts my life, so going to work everyday would have some connection to something I enjoy [which I've never felt with work before]
5) I can stop saying I went to the University of Rochester! 

Note: I hope you can sense my sarcasm and I am actually so proud to have gone to the UofR. I genuinely would not change my mind about where to go to undergrad if I could back in time. Could not reccommend that insitution enough.

As with most trade-offs, there are cons, but I'll hold off on them. Right now, I feel very split about this decision, but I am thinking about applying and pushing back thinking more deeply until I hear back from the schools I'm interested in. But as for now, let's sit on it.
