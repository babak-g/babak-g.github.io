I want to preface; I have no intentions of giving any advice, nor do I want to. I don't think I have much knowledge in this area, and this space is solely a platform for me write down my experience and reflect on it.

I'll start this off by embarking on my health journey in August 2019. I just moved to Seattle; started my big time, fresh SWE job out of college, and just recovered from an ACL reconstruction surgery a month prior. As with any  change, it's often a catalyst to change other things about one's life. For me, I decided to prioritize  my health. Now, what does that even mean? At the time, "healthy" as a concept meant a multitude of things to me.

1. "Looking good" to society

How to grow broad shoulders and thin my waist? How to get a 6-pack? How to make my legs look skinny in jeans?

2. Quantitative strength metrics

Bench pressing XXX lbs, ability to do X pull ups.

I will expand on this later, but this was my motivation. Getting validation that I look good, being able to tell others I lift X amount, and lastly looking at myself in the mirror and not feeling like a piece of shit. My approach at becoming "healthy" consisted of MyFitnessPal, lifting, and some cardio in my apartment gym. My eating habits were much along the lines: "If I had calories left in MyFitnessPal, then it was okay to eat". My foods typically consisted of Cheesecake Factory Skinnylicious menu, Trader Joe's frozen meals, and candy bags from the convenient store across the street.

I was making progress, I got down to around 185-190lbs after 2-3 months. I felt great! People were telling me I looked good, I felt light, and I even believed my knee was getting better since I had less weight on them. I went home for the holidays and immediately gained all the weight back I lost in less than a month, and I slowly crawled back up to ~215 pounds. 

I was defeated. Every time I woke up, it felt like people who judged me were right. 

I am not bringing these memories up in hopes of being felt sorry for, but more to self-realize these moments did really bother me and it's time for me to let them go.

1) Being told I tore my ACL because I was overweight.
2) Being told to put my shirt back on at the beach.
3) Being told comments everyday about everything I ate.

I first want to reflect that these were all things that were told to me. Either by loved ones or close acquaintances, which made it hurt more. The environment of constant judgment about how I exercised or what I ate really hurt and it took me too long to realize. These memory fragments caused me to build really unhealthy eating & working-out habits.

I started with religiously using Calories In/Calories Out. I was doing cardio for an hour everyday, minimizing as many meals as possible. I deprived myself of food so much, I'd binge frequently, then have the mindset..."Since I ate double my calories today,  I just won't eat tomorrow". It was bad, but it worked in respect to getting to a lighter weight.

Every week I would look in the  mirror and just say "If I lost 5 more pounds, I would see abs and be so happy with how I'd look". At one point I was 190 lbs, then 185lbs, then 180lbs, then 175 pounds. 

Still no abs.

Eventually I got to 168 and I was energy-deprived walking up steps. I'd watch mukbang videos at night since I'd be so hungry and wanted to experience eating through someone else, and on top of all this, my hormones were all over the place... but I was lean. Once, I got here I kind of had a mid-20s life crisis. Why am I doing this to myself? Did societal judgment really cause all this? Maybe, but I will say I am glad this experience happened. I think this whole journey taught me more about ignoring judgment than it did about health.

Rather than look good to look good, I started researching what things worked for my body and what didn't. Health is so subjective, I hate it. But what I did love was feeling good. Finding how to do that was the hard part for me.

Growing up eating so much sugar and finishing everything on my plate ingrained interesting habits. These habits aren't bad, but they did cause me to feel not how I wanted to, so I sought for a change. I can't stress enough that listening to my body and making data-driven decisions [I know corp-speak cringe] to reach my goals were quintessential. 

But what was my "health" goal? Someone asked me this at work one day and I felt angry. I said I wanted to be strong, and then immediately after they said if I wanted to look good, they could "put me on". This was the day where I decided I hated this health goal question. I learned from exchange that rather than chasing quantifiables, I wanted to chase being in the best state I could be in.

I've tried so many diets to find the answer: Keto, Carnivore, Mediterranean, Intermittent fasting, etc. But I genuinely think tailoring myself to these 3 things:

(1) eating what I want to eat
(2) eating what makes me feel good  
(3) putting myself in an environment that I want to be in

did it for me. I currently don't track calories, maximize protein, eat carbs when I want energy, and have some fats scattered in my meals. And this works great for me, I feel super attentive during the day, I sleep extremely well, and most importantly, I feel the relationships around me growing stronger. This journey destroyed me to some degree, but I couldn't be happier with my story.

I promised that I'd give no advice, but if you ever need support. I am here.
